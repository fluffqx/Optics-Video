Week 4 introduces a powerful systematic method for tracing rays through any optical system — matrix optics, also called the ray transfer matrix method.

The idea is elegant. We represent a ray by a two-component column vector: its height y above the optical axis, and its angle theta with respect to the axis. Then every optical element — lens, mirror, free space — is represented by a two-by-two matrix. To trace a ray through the whole system, we simply multiply the matrices together.

This is why computers are so good at optical design. Matrix multiplication is fast and exact, and the same formalism works for any sequence of elements.

There are four fundamental matrices. The translation matrix for free propagation over distance d has ones on the diagonal and d in the top right — the height increases by d times theta, while the angle is unchanged.

The thin lens matrix has a minus one over f in the lower left — the lens changes the ray angle but not its height at the lens.

The refraction matrix at a spherical surface accounts for both the change in angle due to the surface curvature and the ratio of refractive indices.

The mirror matrix looks like the lens matrix with minus two over R in the lower left.

To find the system matrix for a complete optical system, multiply the individual matrices from right to left — the first element the ray encounters goes on the rightmost position.

From the system matrix elements A, B, C, D, you can extract all cardinal points of the system: the effective focal length is minus one over C, and the principal planes can be located from A and D.

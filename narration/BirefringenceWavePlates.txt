Bennett Section 6.3 discusses birefringence — also known as double refraction — in crystalline materials. A birefringent crystal has an ordered atomic structure that makes the index of refraction anisotropic: it depends on both the propagation direction and the polarisation direction.

Calcite (CaCO3) is the classic example, with a hexagonal crystal structure and one optic axis. An unpolarised beam incident normally on a cleavage plane refracts into two beams: the ordinary ray, which obeys Snell's law with index n-o equals 1.6584 at 589 nanometres, and the extraordinary ray, which does not obey Snell's law and travels with a direction-dependent index n-e equals 1.4849 at 589 nanometres. The ordinary ray is polarised perpendicular to the principal section; the extraordinary ray is polarised within the principal section.

A wave plate, also called a retarder, is a slab of birefringent material cut so that the optic axis is parallel to the face of the slab, as shown in Bennett Figure 6.10(c). A beam normally incident on such a slab resolves into two components: one polarised along the optic axis (travelling with index n-e) and one polarised perpendicular to it (travelling with n-o). Since the two components travel at different speeds, one accumulates more phase than the other after passing through the slab.

The relative phase shift is derived in Bennett Equation 6.8. The n-e component travels optical distance n-e times d, and the n-o component travels n-o times d. The phase of a wave changes by k times distance, where k equals 2 pi over lambda-naught. The relative phase shift is therefore Delta-phi equals 2 pi d over lambda-naught times the quantity n-o minus n-e. The axis with the smaller index — n-e for calcite since n-e is less than n-o — is the fast axis because light on it travels faster.

A quarter-wave plate is designed so that d times the absolute value of n-e minus n-o equals lambda-naught over 4, as given in Bennett Equation 6.10. The relative phase shift is then pi over 2. The minimum thickness for quartz at 589 nanometres is, from Bennett Example 6.3: d equals lambda-naught divided by 4 times the absolute value of n-e minus n-o equals 589 nanometres divided by 4 times the absolute value of 1.5533 minus 1.5442 equals 16.2 micrometres. Such thin plates are fragile, so a more practical thickness uses a higher value of m — Bennett Example 6.3 gives d equals 1.30 centimetres for m equals 200, which provides the same quarter-wave phase shift but is much more robust.

A half-wave plate satisfies d times the absolute value of n-e minus n-o equals lambda-naught over 2, from Bennett Equation 6.11. The phase shift is pi. A half-wave plate with fast axis horizontal negates the y-component of the electric field. If the incident polarisation is at angle theta from the fast axis, the output polarisation is rotated by 2 theta. Using theta equals 45 degrees rotates the polarisation by 90 degrees — something that cannot be achieved with a single linear polariser, as Bennett notes in Section 6.4.

Bennett Example 6.4 shows what happens when left-circularly polarised light passes through a quarter-wave plate with horizontal fast axis. The incident beam has Jones vector 1, i. The QWP matrix adds an additional i to the y-component: the output is 1, i times i equals 1, minus 1. This is linear polarisation at minus 45 degrees. The QWP has converted circular back to linear.

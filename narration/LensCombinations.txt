For two thin lenses separated by distance d, Bennett Section 4.4 gives the procedure: apply the thin lens equation to the first lens to find its image, then use that image as the object for the second lens.

If the first lens has focal length f-one and the object is at distance s-o-one, then s-i-one satisfies one over s-o-one plus one over s-i-one equals one over f-one. The object distance for the second lens is s-o-two equals d minus s-i-one. If s-i-one is greater than d — the first image falls beyond the second lens — then s-o-two is negative, meaning a virtual object for the second lens.

Apply the thin lens equation to the second lens: one over s-o-two plus one over s-i-two equals one over f-two. The total magnification is m-total equals m-one times m-two equals minus s-i-one over s-o-one times minus s-i-two over s-o-two.

For lenses in contact with d equals zero, the effective focal length satisfies one over f-eff equals one over f-one plus one over f-two, which is equivalent to P-eff equals P-one plus P-two. The powers add directly. This is why optometrists write prescriptions in dioptres — combining lenses is just addition.

For two lenses separated by distance d, Bennett gives the general formula: one over f-eff equals one over f-one plus one over f-two minus d over f-one f-two. The last term is the correction for separation. For two identical converging lenses each of focal length f separated by d equals f, we get one over f-eff equals 1/f plus 1/f minus f over f squared equals 2/f minus 1/f equals 1/f. The focal length is unchanged — this is the 4-f relay configuration used in Fourier filtering and spatial light modulator systems.

The plane wave solutions to Maxwell's equations in vacuum — fields of the form E equals E-naught times e to the i k-vector dot r minus omega t — have several remarkable and non-obvious properties that follow from the equations themselves.

First, electromagnetic waves are transverse. The electric and magnetic fields are both perpendicular to the direction of propagation. This is not an assumption — it follows from div E equals zero and div B equals zero applied to a plane wave. If E had a component along the propagation direction, the divergence would be nonzero, violating Gauss's law. So E must be purely transverse.

Second, E and B are perpendicular to each other. From Faraday's law for a plane wave, k-vector cross E-vector equals omega times B-vector. Since the cross product is perpendicular to E, B must be perpendicular to E. Furthermore, B is in the direction of k-hat cross E-hat, so the three vectors E, B, and k form a right-handed coordinate system.

Third, the magnitudes of E and B are related by E equals c times B in vacuum. This follows from the same Faraday relation: the magnitude of k-vector cross E equals k times E equals omega times B, so B equals k E over omega equals E over v-phase equals E over c.

Fourth, E and B oscillate in phase — they reach their maxima and minima simultaneously. This is a consequence of the real relationship B equals k-hat cross E divided by c: the phase of B is the same as the phase of E.

Fifth, in a material medium with refractive index n, the wave speed becomes c over n, and the wavelength in the medium is lambda over n. The frequency remains unchanged — only the wavelength and speed change when light enters a medium. This is because the boundary conditions require the frequency to be continuous across any interface.

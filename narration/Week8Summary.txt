Here are all the Fabry-Pérot formulas collected in one place, with Bennett equation numbers.

The Airy function for transmitted intensity from Bennett Equation 7.89: I-t equals I-naught divided by 1 plus F sine squared delta over 2. The coefficient of finesse is F equals 4R over 1 minus R squared. The round-trip phase is delta equals 4 pi n d cosine theta over lambda-naught for a cavity of length d filled with medium n at incidence angle theta.

At resonance, delta equals 2 pi N for integer N: I-t equals I-naught. Complete transmission regardless of R. Between resonances: I-t approaches I-naught over 1 plus F, which is approximately I-naught over F for large F.

The finesse from Bennett Section 7.10: script-F equals pi root R over 1 minus R. This equals the free spectral range divided by the FWHM of each transmission peak. Also: script-F equals pi root F over 2. For R equals 0.9: F equals 360, script-F equals 29.8. For R equals 0.99: F equals 39600, script-F equals 312.

The order number at resonance: N equals 2nd cosine theta over lambda-naught. For normal incidence: N equals 2nd over lambda-naught.

The resolving power from Bennett Section 7.10: RP equals lambda over delta-lambda-min equals N times script-F. For the example of Bennett's Example 7.11 with N equals 1313 and script-F equals 29.8: RP equals 39100.

The free spectral range in wavelength: delta-lambda-FSR equals lambda-naught squared over 2nd equals lambda-naught over N. In frequency: delta-nu-FSR equals c over 2nd. This is the cavity mode spacing and determines the allowed longitudinal modes of a laser.

The coherence length of the source must exceed 2nd for fringes to be observable. Critical warning: never substitute F in place of script-F in the resolving power formula — they differ by a factor of pi over 2 times root F, which for R equals 0.9 is about 15. This is the most common Fabry-Pérot exam error.

The divergence of a vector field F, written as del dot F, measures how much the field spreads outward from a point. In Cartesian coordinates, del dot F equals partial F-x over partial x plus partial F-y over partial y plus partial F-z over partial z. Positive divergence at a point means that more field lines flow out of a small volume around that point than flow in — there is a source. Negative divergence means a sink. Zero divergence means whatever flows in also flows out: the field is source-free. Gauss's law for E, del dot E equals rho over epsilon-naught, says that charges are the sources and sinks of the electric field.

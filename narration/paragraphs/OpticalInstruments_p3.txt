A compound microscope uses two lenses: a short-focal-length objective and a longer-focal-length eyepiece. The objective forms a real, inverted, highly magnified intermediate image. The eyepiece then acts as a simple magnifier for that intermediate image. The total angular magnification is M equals minus L over f-objective times 25 centimetres over f-eyepiece, where L is the tube length — the distance from the back focal point of the objective to the front focal point of the eyepiece. Typical values: f-objective equals 4 millimetres, f-eyepiece equals 25 millimetres, L equals 160 millimetres, giving M equals minus 160 over 4 times 25 over 25 equals minus 40. The image is inverted and 40 times larger.

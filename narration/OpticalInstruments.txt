All optical instruments reduce to combinations of lenses and mirrors. Understanding the operating principle of each one clarifies the optical design choices.

A magnifying glass uses a single converging lens to create a virtual, upright, magnified image of an object placed just inside the focal length. The angular magnification is 25 centimetres divided by f, where 25 centimetres is the conventional near-point distance. A 5-centimetre focal length magnifying glass gives 5-times magnification.

A compound microscope uses two lenses: a short-focal-length objective and a longer-focal-length eyepiece. The objective forms a real, inverted, highly magnified intermediate image. The eyepiece then acts as a simple magnifier for that intermediate image. The total angular magnification is M equals minus L over f-objective times 25 centimetres over f-eyepiece, where L is the tube length — the distance from the back focal point of the objective to the front focal point of the eyepiece. Typical values: f-objective equals 4 millimetres, f-eyepiece equals 25 millimetres, L equals 160 millimetres, giving M equals minus 160 over 4 times 25 over 25 equals minus 40. The image is inverted and 40 times larger.

An astronomical refracting telescope has a long-focal-length objective and a short-focal-length eyepiece, separated by f-objective plus f-eyepiece. Parallel light from a distant star enters and the objective forms a real image at its back focal point. The eyepiece magnifies this image. The angular magnification is minus f-objective over f-eyepiece. A 100-centimetre objective with a 2-centimetre eyepiece gives 50-times magnification.

The human eye has a total focal length of about 22 millimetres in air and can focus objects from 25 centimetres to infinity by changing the shape of its crystalline lens. The retina is a curved photosensitive surface 22 millimetres from the optical system. Nearsightedness (myopia) means the focal length is too short: distant images form in front of the retina. Farsightedness (hyperopia) means the focal length is too long: near images would form behind the retina. Astigmatism means the focal length differs in different planes, producing distorted images.

Dispersion is the variation of the phase velocity — and therefore the refractive index — with frequency. Bennett covers dispersion in Section 3.9 using the Lorentz oscillator model.

In a non-dispersive medium, all frequencies travel at the same speed and a wave packet propagates without distortion. In a dispersive medium, different frequencies travel at different speeds and a pulse spreads out as it propagates.

The refractive index is defined as n equals c over v-phase. In a dispersive medium, n depends on frequency. For most optical glasses in the visible spectrum, n increases as frequency increases — this is called normal dispersion. Violet light has a higher index than red light, so it travels more slowly through glass and is refracted more strongly. A prism separates white light into a spectrum because of this frequency dependence of n.

Bennett's Lorentz oscillator model treats electrons in atoms as bound harmonic oscillators with natural resonance frequencies. When driven by a light wave, the electron responds with an oscillation that has a frequency-dependent phase relative to the driving field. This phase lag modifies the propagation speed of the wave in the medium.

Well below resonance, the oscillators respond nearly in phase with the field and n is real, slightly greater than one, and increases slowly with frequency. Near resonance, the response changes rapidly — n goes through a rapid variation and absorption becomes strong. Well above resonance, the oscillators cannot follow the driving field and n approaches one from below — which means the phase velocity exceeds c. This does not violate relativity because information cannot be transmitted at phase velocity.

The group velocity in a dispersive medium is c divided by the quantity n plus omega times dn d omega, from Bennett Section 7.7. The second term is the dispersion correction. For normal dispersion where dn d omega is positive, the group velocity is less than c over n. Energy and signals travel more slowly than the wave crests.

Dispersion in optical fibres limits data transmission rates. A short pulse containing many frequencies broadens as it propagates because each frequency component travels at a slightly different speed. For a single-mode fibre at 1550 nanometres, the dispersion coefficient is about 17 picoseconds per nanometre per kilometre. A pulse with a spectral width of one nanometre broadens by 17 picoseconds per kilometre of propagation. Over 1000 kilometres of undersea cable, this becomes 17 nanoseconds of broadening — limiting the pulse rate to below about 60 gigabits per second for a single channel.

Dispersion-shifted fibres and dispersion-compensating fibres can greatly reduce this limitation by engineering the waveguide geometry to counteract the material dispersion.

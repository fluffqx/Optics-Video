Dispersion is the dependence of the refractive index on frequency. It is responsible for the rainbow spectrum from a glass prism, the colour fringing in cheap lenses, and the pulse broadening that limits data rates in optical fibres.

The refractive index n is defined as c over v, the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the phase velocity in the medium. In a non-dispersive medium, n is a constant and all frequencies travel at the same speed. In a dispersive medium, n depends on frequency and different colours travel at different speeds.

The Lorentz oscillator model gives a microscopic understanding of dispersion. Electrons in atoms behave as bound harmonic oscillators with characteristic natural frequencies. When a light wave drives these oscillators, the response depends on how close the driving frequency is to the natural resonance. Well below resonance, the oscillators follow the driving field with a small phase lag, and the refractive index is real and greater than one. Near resonance, the phase lag becomes large, and the index shows a rapid change. Well above resonance, the oscillators cannot keep up with the driving field, and the index approaches one from below.

For most optical glasses in the visible range, n increases with frequency — this is called normal dispersion. Violet light has a higher index than red light, so it travels more slowly and is refracted more. A prism bends violet light through a larger angle than red, separating the colours of white light into a spectrum.

The group velocity in a dispersive medium is c divided by n plus omega times dn d omega. The quantity n plus omega dn d omega is sometimes called the group index. For normal dispersion, dn d omega is positive, so the group index is larger than n, and the group velocity is smaller than the phase velocity. Pulses travel more slowly than their carrier crests.

Anomalous dispersion occurs near absorption resonances, where dn d omega becomes negative. The group velocity can apparently exceed c, and this caused confusion for many years about whether signals could travel faster than light. The resolution is that near an absorption resonance, the pulse is so strongly distorted that it does not make sense to track its peak as the signal. The signal velocity — the speed at which information can actually be transmitted — remains at or below c.

The dispersion of optical fibres limits their data-carrying capacity. A short pulse containing many frequencies broadens as it propagates because different frequency components travel at slightly different group velocities. Over a fibre link of thousands of kilometres, this broadening can be severe. Dispersion-compensating fibres, with deliberately opposite dispersion, can partially reverse this broadening and allow higher data rates.

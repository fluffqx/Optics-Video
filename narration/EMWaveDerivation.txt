Bennett derives the electromagnetic wave equation in Appendix B and summarises the result in Section 2.3. The derivation uses two of Maxwell's four equations: Faraday's law and the Ampère-Maxwell law.

Start with Faraday's law: curl of E equals minus partial B over partial t. Take the curl of both sides: curl of curl of E equals minus partial over partial t of curl of B. Substitute the Ampère-Maxwell law, curl of B equals mu-naught epsilon-naught times partial E over partial t, giving: curl of curl of E equals minus mu-naught epsilon-naught times the second time derivative of E.

Apply the vector identity curl of curl of F equals grad of div of F minus del-squared of F. In vacuum, div of E equals zero from Gauss's law, so the first term vanishes. The result is: del-squared E equals mu-naught epsilon-naught times partial squared E over partial t squared. This is the wave equation for E with wave speed v equals one over the square root of mu-naught epsilon-naught.

Substituting the values from Bennett: mu-naught equals 4 pi times ten to the minus 7 henries per metre, epsilon-naught equals 8.854 times ten to the minus 12 farads per metre. The product mu-naught epsilon-naught equals 1.113 times ten to the minus 17 seconds squared per metre squared. The wave speed is v equals 1 over the square root of 1.113 times ten to the minus 17, which equals 2.998 times ten to the 8 metres per second. This is the measured speed of light to five significant figures.

Maxwell performed this calculation in 1865 using electromagnetic measurements alone, without any optical data. The agreement with the measured speed of light led him to conclude — correctly — that light is an electromagnetic wave. This was one of the great unifications in the history of physics.

Working directly with sines and cosines becomes extremely cumbersome as soon as you start adding multiple waves or computing intensities. The complex representation replaces these trigonometric functions with exponentials, making the algebra vastly simpler. This is not a mathematical trick — it is the standard language of wave optics.

The foundation is Euler's formula: e to the power i theta equals cosine theta plus i times sine theta. This identity connects the exponential function to the trigonometric functions through the imaginary unit i. It can be proved from the Taylor series: expand e to the ix, group the real and imaginary parts, and recognise the series for cosine and sine respectively.

From Euler's formula, cosine theta is the real part of e to the i theta, and sine theta is the imaginary part. This means we can write a wave psi equals A cosine of kx minus omega t as the real part of A times e to the i times kx minus omega t. We define the complex wave psi-tilde as A times e to the i times kx minus omega t. The physical wave is always the real part of this complex quantity.

Why is this better? Because multiplication of complex exponentials is trivial: e to the ia times e to the ib just equals e to the i times a plus b. Adding complex numbers is just adding their real and imaginary parts separately — there are no product-to-sum identities needed, no half-angle formulas, no painful integrals of cosine squared. You do all your algebra with exponentials and take the real part only at the very end.

The intensity is proportional to the modulus squared: I is proportional to the absolute value of psi-tilde squared, which equals psi-tilde times its complex conjugate psi-tilde-star. For our plane wave, this gives A squared — a constant, as expected for a pure sinusoidal wave. The phase information cancels out in the intensity, which is why intensity measurements cannot tell you the phase of a wave.

This is also why interference is so interesting: when two waves add, the intensity of the sum is not simply the sum of the intensities. The cross terms produce the interference pattern. In complex notation: the intensity of psi-one plus psi-two equals the modulus squared of psi-one-tilde plus psi-two-tilde, which expands to give the intensities of each wave plus twice the real part of psi-one-tilde times psi-two-tilde-star. That cross term is the interference term.

Extending to three dimensions, a plane wave propagating in the direction of the wave vector k-vector is written as A times e to the i times k-vector dot r-vector minus omega t. The dot product k-vector dot r-vector extracts the component of position along the propagation direction. Surfaces of constant phase — wavefronts — are the planes where k-vector dot r-vector equals a constant, which are indeed flat planes perpendicular to k-vector.

A spherical wave spreading out from a point source at the origin has the form A divided by r, times e to the i times kr minus omega t. The amplitude decreases as one over r, which ensures that the intensity — proportional to amplitude squared — falls as one over r squared. This is the inverse-square law, and it follows from energy conservation: the total power flowing through any sphere centered on the source must be the same, so the intensity must decrease as the area of the sphere increases, which goes as r squared.

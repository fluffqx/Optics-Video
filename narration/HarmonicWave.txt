The most important special case of a travelling wave is the harmonic wave — a pure sine or cosine oscillation.

Psi of x and t equals A times sine of kx minus omega t plus phi.

Let's go through every symbol carefully, because these appear constantly throughout the course.

A is the amplitude — the maximum value of the wave. For an electric field, it's in volts per metre. For a pressure wave, it would be in Pascals.

k is the wave number, equal to 2 pi divided by the wavelength lambda. It tells you how many radians of oscillation fit into one metre of space. Its units are radians per metre.

Omega is the angular frequency, equal to 2 pi times the frequency f. It tells you how many radians of oscillation happen per second.

Lambda is the wavelength — the distance between two consecutive crests.

f is the ordinary frequency in hertz — how many full cycles per second. Related to the period T by f equals one over T.

v is the phase speed — how fast the wave crests move. It equals omega divided by k, which also equals f times lambda.

And phi is the initial phase offset — it shifts the wave left or right in time.

Let's do a quick example. Given lambda equals 500 nanometres and v equals 3 times 10 to the 8 metres per second — visible green light — we can find all the other quantities.

k equals 2 pi divided by 500 times 10 to the minus 9, which gives us 1.257 times 10 to the 7 radians per metre.

Multiplying by v gives omega equal to 3.77 times 10 to the 15 radians per second.

And the period T equals 2 pi divided by omega, giving 1.67 times 10 to the minus 15 seconds — about 1.7 femtoseconds. Light oscillates extraordinarily fast.

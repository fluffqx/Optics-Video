Radiation pressure is a direct consequence of the fact that light carries momentum. This was predicted by Maxwell in 1865 and first measured experimentally by Lebedev in 1900. It is a subtle but real effect.

The momentum density of an electromagnetic wave is the Poynting vector divided by c-squared, or equivalently the irradiance divided by c-cubed. When this momentum is absorbed by a surface, Newton's second law gives a force, and force per unit area is pressure.

For a perfectly absorbing black surface, the radiation pressure is I over c. For a perfectly reflecting mirror, the photon's momentum reverses completely, so the pressure is twice as large: 2I over c. A partial reflector with reflectivity R has a radiation pressure between these limits: the reflected fraction contributes 2RI over c and the absorbed fraction contributes the remaining contribution.

Solar radiation pressure provides about 9 micropascals at Earth's orbit. While tiny, it is enough to alter satellite orbits over time — this effect must be included in precise orbital calculations. The IKAROS spacecraft launched by JAXA in 2010 was the first to use solar sail propulsion for interplanetary travel, demonstrating that radiation pressure can actually propel spacecraft.

On Earth, optical tweezers use focused laser beams to trap and manipulate microscopic particles — even living cells — using radiation pressure. The particle is pulled toward the focus by the intensity gradient. Nobel Prize 2018 was awarded partly for the development of optical tweezers by Arthur Ashkin.

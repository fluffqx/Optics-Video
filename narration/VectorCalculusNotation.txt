Three differential operators appear throughout Maxwell's equations and the wave equation: the gradient, the divergence, and the curl. Before working with Maxwell's equations, we need a clear physical understanding of what each operator measures.

The gradient of a scalar field phi, written as del phi or grad phi, is a vector that points in the direction of maximum increase of phi and has magnitude equal to the rate of increase in that direction. In Cartesian coordinates, del phi equals the vector of partial phi over partial x, partial phi over partial y, partial phi over partial z. If phi is the temperature distribution in a room, del phi points toward the hottest nearby region. A positive charge in an electric potential phi feels a force proportional to minus del phi — it accelerates away from the gradient direction toward lower potential.

The divergence of a vector field F, written as del dot F, measures how much the field spreads outward from a point. In Cartesian coordinates, del dot F equals partial F-x over partial x plus partial F-y over partial y plus partial F-z over partial z. Positive divergence at a point means that more field lines flow out of a small volume around that point than flow in — there is a source. Negative divergence means a sink. Zero divergence means whatever flows in also flows out: the field is source-free. Gauss's law for E, del dot E equals rho over epsilon-naught, says that charges are the sources and sinks of the electric field.

The curl of a vector field F, written as del cross F, measures the tendency of the field to rotate around a point. Imagine placing a small paddle wheel in a fluid whose velocity is F. If the wheel rotates, the curl is nonzero in the direction of the rotation axis. Zero curl means the field is irrotational. Faraday's law, curl of E equals minus partial B over partial t, says that a time-varying magnetic field drives a curling electric field — the electric field circulates around regions where B is changing.

The Laplacian, written as del squared or nabla squared, is the divergence of the gradient: del squared phi equals del dot del phi. For a scalar field, del squared phi equals the sum of the three second partial derivatives with respect to x, y, and z. It measures how the value of phi at a point compares to the average of phi in the surrounding neighbourhood. The wave equation del squared psi equals one over v-squared times the second time derivative of psi involves the Laplacian acting on a vector field, where it is applied component by component in Cartesian coordinates.

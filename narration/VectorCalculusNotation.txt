The divergence of a vector field F, written as del dot F, measures how much the field spreads outward from a point. Imagine F as a fluid velocity field. A positive divergence at a point means fluid is being created there — it is a source. A negative divergence means fluid is being absorbed — a sink. Zero divergence means the field lines neither originate nor terminate at that point: whatever flows in also flows out.

The curl of a vector field F, written as del cross F, measures how much the field rotates around a point. Think of placing a small paddle wheel in the fluid field. If the wheel rotates, the curl is nonzero in the direction of the rotation axis. Zero curl means the field is irrotational — no net rotation around any point.

The Laplacian, written as del squared F, is the three-dimensional generalization of the second derivative. For a scalar field, it equals the sum of the second partial derivatives with respect to x, y, and z. It appears in the wave equation and measures how the value at a point differs from the average of values in its neighbourhood. A positive Laplacian means the value is below its surroundings — it will tend to increase. A negative Laplacian means it is above its surroundings — it will tend to decrease.

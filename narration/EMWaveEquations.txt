Now watch what happens when we take the curl of Faraday's law and substitute in the Ampere-Maxwell law.

After the algebra — which you should work through at least once — we get a wave equation for the electric field: nabla squared E equals epsilon naught mu naught times the second time derivative of E.

And identically for B: nabla squared B equals epsilon naught mu naught times the second time derivative of B.

Comparing with the standard wave equation, we immediately read off the wave speed: v equals 1 over the square root of epsilon naught mu naught. Plug in the numbers, and you get exactly 2.998 times 10 to the 8 metres per second. The speed of light.

This is one of the most profound results in all of physics. Maxwell showed that light is nothing other than oscillating electric and magnetic fields.

In a material with relative permittivity K_E, the wave speed becomes c over the square root of K_E, so the refractive index is n equals the square root of K_E.

Now, the electric and magnetic fields in an electromagnetic wave are not independent. They're perpendicular to each other, and both are perpendicular to the direction of propagation. The wave is transverse.

The magnitudes are related by E equals v times B in a medium, or E equals c times B in vacuum.

Let's work through an example. If the electric field is E naught in the x-hat direction, propagating in the z direction, what is B?

The propagation direction is z-hat. Using the relation B equals k-vector cross E divided by omega, we get k over omega times z-hat cross x-hat, times E naught. Z-hat cross x-hat is y-hat. So B points in the y direction, with magnitude E naught over c. The three vectors — E, B, and k — form a right-handed coordinate system.

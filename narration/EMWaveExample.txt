Let's work through Bennett's approach to finding the magnetic field associated with a given electric field, following Section 2.3.1.

Given: a plane electromagnetic wave propagating in the positive z-direction with electric field E-vector equals E-naught times x-hat times e to the i times kz minus omega t. Find the magnetic field B.

From Faraday's law for a plane wave: the relationship between E and B for a plane wave is B equals k-hat cross E divided by the phase velocity v. The propagation direction is k-hat equals z-hat. The cross product z-hat cross x-hat equals y-hat by the right-hand rule. So B-vector equals E-naught over c times y-hat times e to the i times kz minus omega t.

Verify all four required properties of an electromagnetic wave from Bennett Section 2.3.1. First, E is transverse: E points in x-hat, which is perpendicular to the propagation direction z-hat. Second, B is transverse: B points in y-hat, also perpendicular to z-hat. Third, E is perpendicular to B: x-hat is perpendicular to y-hat. Fourth, E cross B points in the propagation direction: x-hat cross y-hat equals z-hat, which is the propagation direction z-hat. All four properties confirmed.

The magnitude ratio E over B equals E-naught over E-naught over c equals c. This is Bennett's result from Section 2.3.1: E equals c B in vacuum. The electric and magnetic fields have the same spatial and temporal phase factors, confirming they oscillate in phase.

Now compute the irradiance from Bennett Equation 2.33: I equals one half times epsilon-naught times c times E-naught squared. For E-naught equals 1000 volts per metre: I equals 0.5 times 8.854 times ten to the minus 12 times 3 times ten to the 8 times ten to the 6 equals 1327 watts per square metre.

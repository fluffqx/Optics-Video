Here are Maxwell's four equations in matter. I will present each one with its physical interpretation — not just the mathematical statement, but what it is actually telling us about the electromagnetic field.

Gauss's Law for the electric field states that the divergence of E equals rho-free divided by epsilon, where rho-free is the density of free charges and epsilon is the permittivity of the medium. In words: electric field lines begin on positive charges and end on negative charges. In regions with no charges, the divergence is zero — field lines neither originate nor terminate there. This is a statement about electric monopoles: positive and negative charges are the sources and sinks of the electric field.

Gauss's Law for the magnetic field states that the divergence of B is always zero — everywhere, without exception. This is a statement about the non-existence of magnetic monopoles. There is no magnetic equivalent of a charge — no isolated north or south pole. Every magnetic field line must form a closed loop. A field line that enters any closed surface must exit it. This is one of the great fundamental laws of nature — it has been tested to extraordinary precision and no violation has ever been found.

Faraday's Law states that the curl of E equals minus the partial derivative of B with respect to time. A time-varying magnetic field creates a curling electric field. The negative sign is Lenz's law: the induced electric field drives currents that create magnetic fields opposing the change. This is the principle behind electric generators, transformers, and inductors. Without Faraday's law, there would be no electrical power grid.

The Ampère-Maxwell Law states that the curl of B equals mu times J-free plus mu times epsilon times the time derivative of E. Electric currents create curling magnetic fields — this is Ampère's original law, which explains why wires carrying current are surrounded by magnetic fields. Maxwell's crucial addition is the displacement current term: mu epsilon times partial E partial t. A changing electric field also creates a curling magnetic field, even in the absence of any real current. This term was Maxwell's insight that made the equations self-consistent and, as a consequence, predicted electromagnetic waves travelling at the speed of light.

In vacuum with no charges and no currents, the equations simplify dramatically. The divergences of E and B are both zero. The curls are coupled: curl of E equals minus dB dt, and curl of B equals mu-naught epsilon-naught times dE dt. These last two equations are remarkable: a changing E creates a curling B, and a changing B creates a curling E. This mutual sustaining of electric and magnetic oscillations is the electromagnetic wave.

Taking the curl of Faraday's law and substituting the Ampère-Maxwell law yields the wave equation for E: del squared E equals mu-naught epsilon-naught times the second time derivative of E. Comparing this with the standard wave equation, the wave speed is one over the square root of mu-naught epsilon-naught. When Maxwell computed this from the known values of these constants, he obtained 3.107 times ten to the eighth metres per second — within experimental error of the measured speed of light. Light is an electromagnetic wave.

Geometric optics applies when the wavelength of light is much smaller than all the apertures, lenses, and optical elements in the system. Under this condition, diffraction effects are negligible and we can treat light as travelling in straight lines called rays. The ray is perpendicular to the wavefront of the wave at every point.

The paraxial approximation further simplifies the analysis. We consider only rays that travel at small angles to the optical axis — the main axis of the optical system. For small angles theta in radians, sine theta approximately equals theta and cosine theta approximately equals one. This linearises all the optical equations, making them analytically tractable.

The domain of geometric optics covers most of everyday optics: eyeglasses, camera lenses, binoculars, telescopes, microscopes, projectors. It breaks down when apertures become comparable to the wavelength — which brings us to diffraction in Week 7 — and when coherence effects matter — which brings us to interference in Week 6. But within its domain of validity, geometric optics is exact.

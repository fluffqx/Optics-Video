Huygens proposed in 1678 that every point on a wavefront acts as the source of a secondary spherical wavelet. The new wavefront at a later time is the envelope of all these secondary wavelets. This principle correctly predicts reflection, refraction, and diffraction, though it was not properly derived from wave theory until Fresnel and Kirchhoff provided rigorous mathematical formulations in the 19th century.

In Young's double-slit experiment, we had two discrete Huygens sources — the two slits. The interference pattern was the superposition of two spherical waves. In single-slit diffraction, we have an infinite number of Huygens sources distributed continuously across the slit aperture. The diffraction pattern is the superposition of an infinite number of spherical waves, one from each point in the aperture.

The Fraunhofer approximation — also called the far-field approximation — applies when the screen is far enough from the aperture that the wavefronts arriving at the screen can be treated as plane waves. The condition is L much greater than a squared over lambda, where a is the aperture size and L is the distance to the screen. For a 0.1 millimetre slit, 500 nanometre light, and a screen 2 metres away: a squared over lambda equals ten to the minus eight over five times ten to the minus seven equals 0.02 metres. Since 2 metres is much larger than 0.02 metres, Fraunhofer applies.

In the Fraunhofer regime, the diffraction integral simplifies to a Fourier transform. The electric field pattern at angle theta is proportional to the Fourier transform of the aperture function, evaluated at spatial frequency u equals sine theta over lambda. This Fourier relationship between aperture and diffraction pattern is one of the most fundamental results in wave optics. It means that a wider aperture gives a narrower diffraction pattern, and vice versa — precisely the wave-optics uncertainty principle.

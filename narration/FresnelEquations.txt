Snell's law tells us directions. The Fresnel equations tell us amplitudes — how much of the electric field is reflected and how much is transmitted.

The key insight is that polarisation matters at an interface. We split the electric field into two independent components.

S-polarisation — sometimes called TE or transverse electric — has the electric field perpendicular to the plane of incidence. If you imagine the ray diagram drawn on paper, the s-polarised field points out of the paper toward you.

P-polarisation — sometimes called TM or transverse magnetic — has the electric field lying in the plane of incidence, parallel to the page.

These two polarisations reflect and transmit differently, and the Fresnel equations give us the amplitude reflection coefficient r and amplitude transmission coefficient t for each.

For s-polarisation: r_perp equals n_i cosine theta_i minus n_t cosine theta_t, all divided by n_i cosine theta_i plus n_t cosine theta_t.

For p-polarisation: r_parallel equals n_t cosine theta_i minus n_i cosine theta_t, all divided by n_t cosine theta_i plus n_i cosine theta_t.

At normal incidence — when the light hits straight on — both polarisations give the same result: r equals n_i minus n_t over n_i plus n_t. This is a very useful formula to remember.

Let's work through a full example. Air to glass, n_i equals 1.0, n_t equals 1.5, theta_i equals 40 degrees.

Step 1: Snell's law gives sine theta_t equals 1.0 over 1.5 times sine 40 degrees equals 0.4285, so theta_t equals 25.37 degrees.

Step 2: compute the cosines. Cosine 40 is 0.766, cosine 25.37 is 0.9036.

Step 3: plug into the Fresnel equations. r_perp comes out to minus 0.278. The negative sign means a phase flip on reflection. r_parallel comes out to plus 0.119 — a smaller reflection with no phase flip.

The p-polarisation reflects less. This will become important when we discuss Brewster's angle.

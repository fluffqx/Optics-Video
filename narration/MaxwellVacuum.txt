In vacuum with no free charges and no free currents, Maxwell's four equations simplify to their cleanest form. The divergence of E is zero and the divergence of B is zero — there are no sources or sinks of either field anywhere. The curl equations become: curl of E equals minus partial B over partial t, and curl of B equals mu-naught epsilon-naught times partial E over partial t.

These two curl equations are remarkable in their symmetry and their consequences. A spatially varying E field produces a time-varying B field, from Faraday's law. That time-varying B field produces a spatially varying E field, from the Ampère-Maxwell law. The fields sustain each other's oscillation. No medium is required — the mutual induction of the fields is self-sustaining in vacuum.

To find the wave equation, Bennett takes the curl of Faraday's law and substitutes the Ampère-Maxwell law for the curl of B. The result is the electromagnetic wave equation: del-squared E equals mu-naught epsilon-naught times partial squared E over partial t squared, with wave speed c equals one over the square root of mu-naught epsilon-naught.

An identical equation holds for B: del-squared B equals mu-naught epsilon-naught times partial squared B over partial t squared. Both E and B satisfy the same wave equation and propagate at the same speed c. They are not independent — they are coupled by Maxwell's equations, and specifying E at all times completely determines B and vice versa.

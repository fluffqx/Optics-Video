The general formula for two-beam interference starts from the superposition of the two complex fields. Let field one have amplitude E-one and phase phi-one, and field two have amplitude E-two and phase phi-two. The total field is E-one e to the i phi-one plus E-two e to the i phi-two.

The intensity is proportional to the modulus squared of the total field. Expanding: I equals E-one squared plus E-two squared plus two E-one E-two cosine of phi-two minus phi-one. Writing this in terms of the individual intensities I-one and I-two and the phase difference delta equals phi-two minus phi-one: I equals I-one plus I-two plus 2 times the square root of I-one I-two times cosine delta.

The third term — 2 root I-one I-two cosine delta — is the interference term. It oscillates between plus 2 root I-one I-two and minus 2 root I-one I-two as the phase difference delta varies. For equal intensities I-one equals I-two equals I-naught: I equals 4 I-naught cosine squared of delta over 2. The intensity ranges from zero to 4 I-naught.

The phase difference delta comes from two sources. First, the optical path difference — any geometric difference in the distances the two beams travel contributes 2 pi over lambda times the optical path difference to the phase difference. Second, reflection phase shifts — any reflection at an interface where the refractive index increases causes a phase shift of pi.

The fringe spacing — how quickly the pattern alternates between bright and dark — depends on the geometry of the setup and the wavelength. Shorter wavelength gives finer fringes for the same geometry. Larger angle between the beams gives finer fringes. This is the basis of interferometric measurement of wavelength.

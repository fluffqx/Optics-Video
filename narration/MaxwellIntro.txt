Maxwell's equations are the four fundamental laws of classical electromagnetism. James Clerk Maxwell formulated them in the 1860s, unifying electricity, magnetism, and optics into a single coherent theory. Before Maxwell, these seemed like three separate branches of physics. After Maxwell, they were revealed as three aspects of a single underlying reality: the electromagnetic field.

Everything we study in this optics course ultimately derives from Maxwell's equations. The propagation of light, the reflection and refraction at surfaces, the interference of coherent beams, the diffraction through apertures, the operation of the Fabry-Pérot interferometer — all of it follows from these four equations. Understanding Maxwell's equations at a deep level is not optional background knowledge. It is the foundation.

Before writing them down, we need to fix the vector calculus notation. Three differential operators appear repeatedly: the divergence, the curl, and the Laplacian. Let's understand each one physically before seeing the equations.

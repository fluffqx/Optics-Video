Maxwell's equations are the four fundamental laws governing all classical electromagnetic phenomena. James Clerk Maxwell formulated them in their modern form in 1865, unifying what had previously appeared to be three separate branches of physics: electricity, magnetism, and optics. Before Maxwell, these seemed like distinct subjects studied by different methods. After Maxwell, they were revealed as aspects of a single underlying physical reality — the electromagnetic field.

The four equations describe: how electric charges create electric fields, how there are no magnetic monopoles, how time-varying magnetic fields create electric fields, and how electric currents and time-varying electric fields create magnetic fields. The last two equations are coupled: a changing E creates a changing B, and a changing B creates a changing E. This mutual sustaining of oscillations is what allows electromagnetic waves to propagate through empty space.

Everything in this optics course ultimately derives from Maxwell's equations. The propagation of light as a transverse wave, the reflection and refraction described by the Fresnel equations, the interference of coherent beams, the diffraction of waves through apertures, and the operation of the Fabry-Pérot interferometer — all follow from these four equations, the boundary conditions they imply, and the wave solutions they support. Understanding Maxwell's equations at a conceptual level is essential for understanding why optics behaves as it does.

Bennett presents Maxwell's equations in both differential and integral forms in Section 2.2 and Appendix B. We use the differential form throughout this course, as it is most natural for wave problems.

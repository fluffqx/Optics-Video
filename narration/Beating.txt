Beating occurs when two harmonic waves of slightly different angular frequencies omega-one and omega-two are superimposed. Bennett Section 7.2 derives the result using the sum-to-product trigonometric identity.

Consider two waves of equal amplitude A: psi equals A cosine omega-one t plus A cosine omega-two t. Using the identity cosine u plus cosine v equals 2 cosine of u plus v over 2 times cosine of u minus v over 2, the superposition becomes: 2A cosine of omega-one plus omega-two over 2 times t, times cosine of omega-one minus omega-two over 2 times t.

The first factor, cosine of the average frequency omega-bar equals omega-one plus omega-two over 2, is the fast carrier oscillation. The second factor, cosine of half the difference frequency, is the slow envelope modulation. The envelope amplitude oscillates between 2A and minus 2A at the beat angular frequency omega-beat equals omega-one minus omega-two divided by 2. Since the perceived intensity goes as amplitude squared, the intensity oscillates at the full beat frequency: f-beat equals the absolute value of f-one minus f-two.

This is Bennett's result. The beat frequency equals the absolute difference of the two component frequencies.

In acoustics: two guitar strings at 440 and 443 hertz produce 3 beats per second. Musicians use beating to tune — they listen for the beats to slow down and disappear as the second string approaches the correct pitch.

In optics: heterodyne detection uses beating between a signal beam at frequency omega-s and a strong local oscillator at frequency omega-lo. The detector produces an output at the beat frequency omega-s minus omega-lo, which can be in the radio frequency or microwave range and is easily processed electronically. Heterodyne detection is used in coherent optical communication systems, laser Doppler velocimetry, and precision spectroscopy.

The coherence requirement: beating is only observable if the two wave trains overlap in time. The observation time must be shorter than the coherence time of each source. For two laser beams with linewidths of 1 megahertz, the coherence time is one microsecond, and beats can be observed for path length differences of up to 300 metres.

Bennett Section 1.7 generalises the wave equation to three spatial dimensions. The three-dimensional wave equation is del-squared psi equals one over v-squared times partial squared psi over partial t squared, where del-squared is the Laplacian operator. The equation has the same structure as the 1D case and the same solutions, but in three dimensions.

A three-dimensional plane wave propagating in the direction of the wave vector k-vector is given by Bennett Equation 1.43: psi-tilde of r-vector and t equals A e to the i times k-vector dot r-vector minus omega t. The dot product k-vector dot r-vector selects the component of position along the propagation direction. Surfaces of constant phase — the wavefronts — are planes perpendicular to k-vector. The wave number is the magnitude of k-vector: k equals the magnitude of k-vector equals 2 pi over lambda.

A spherical wave from a point source at the origin has the form A over r times e to the i times kr minus omega t, where r is the distance from the source. The amplitude decreases as one over r. Since irradiance is proportional to amplitude squared, the irradiance falls as one over r-squared — the inverse-square law. This follows directly from energy conservation: the total power flowing through any sphere centred on the source is constant, and the sphere's area increases as r-squared, so the irradiance must decrease as one over r-squared.

The wave vector k-vector for a three-dimensional plane wave has components k-x, k-y, k-z and magnitude k equals omega over v. The direction of k-vector is the propagation direction. Writing k-vector equals k-x x-hat plus k-y y-hat plus k-z z-hat, the phase at position r-vector equals x x-hat plus y y-hat plus z z-hat is k-x times x plus k-y times y plus k-z times z minus omega t. Surfaces where this phase is constant are the wavefronts.
